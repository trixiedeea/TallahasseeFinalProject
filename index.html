Welcome to Tallahassee

This Is Tallahassee

So here's the thing about Tallahassee: it's hot, it's hilly (yes, hilly — this is not the Florida you're picturing), and it is stubbornly, defiantly not a beach town. If you moved here expecting palm trees and boardwalks, you got oak canopies, red clay, and a humidity level that personally offends you every August. Summers hover somewhere between "swamp" and "the surface of Venus," while winters are so mild that locals wear parkas the one week it hits 45 degrees. Hurricane season keeps everyone humble. You will, at some point, evacuate for a storm that curves away at the last minute, and you will not be mad about it because free days off.

Architecturally, Tallahassee is a weird little layer cake. You've got the Old Capitol — a domed, striped-awning Greek Revival building that looks like it belongs on a postcard — sitting directly in the shadow of the New Capitol, a 22-story high-rise that looks like it belongs in an insurance commercial. Somebody, at some point, decided not to tear the old one down when they built the new one, and honestly, good call, because now tourists get to take a photo of "government past" literally elbow-to-elbow with "government present." Add in FSU's stadium looming over one side of downtown and a historic district full of shotgun houses on the other, and you get a skyline that can't decide what decade it's in.

And then there are the famous residents — or, more accurately, the famous people who briefly passed through and left. Ralph Waldo Emerson visited once and called the place "a grotesque place of land speculators and desperados," which, frankly, feels like a review Tallahassee has never fully lived down. Achille Murat — actual nephew of Napoleon Bonaparte — moved here, named his plantation an anagram of "Naples," and became best friends with Emerson, which is a sentence that should really be its own movie. More recently, Andrew Gillum went from Tallahassee mayor to nearly-Florida-governor to national headline for reasons that had absolutely nothing to do with policy. This is a town that produces main characters.

Government, Gossip, and the Occasional Scandal

You cannot talk about Tallahassee without talking about politics, because politics is basically the local industry. This is the seat of all three branches of Florida's government — the Governor's Office, the Legislature, and the Florida Supreme Court all operate within a few blocks of each other, which means half the city's economy runs on lobbyists, lawyers, consultants, and people whose job title is some variation of "government affairs." During legislative session, the population effectively doubles with staffers, reporters, and interest groups, and every restaurant downtown suddenly fills up with people in suits arguing about tax policy over lunch.

It's also a genuinely more purple pocket than you'd expect from a capital deep in a red state. Leon County tends to vote more Democratic than most of North Florida, thanks in large part to two university populations and a massive state workforce, which makes for some entertaining political whiplash — deep-red statehouse, blue-leaning host city. Add in student activism, and you get a town with a real protest tradition, going all the way back to the Tallahassee Bus Boycott in the 1950s, one of the earliest organized challenges to segregated transit in the country.

Now, the scandals. Because there are always scandals when you cram this many politicians into one zip code. Andrew Gillum's post-mayoral, post-almost-governor chapter turned into a genuinely wild tabloid saga involving a hotel room, some substances, and a lot of very uncomfortable press conferences — the kind of story that made Tallahassee trend on Twitter for reasons that had nothing to do with legislation. And he's far from the only local official to have had a "well, that escalated" moment; state capitals just tend to be scandal factories, and Tallahassee has kept its factory running at a healthy clip for two centuries.

Honestly, the scandal-to-population ratio here might be the highest in the state, and that's saying something in Florida. Maybe it's the concentration of power, maybe it's the small-town-meets-state-capital energy where everybody knows everybody's business, or maybe politicians are just like this everywhere and Tallahassee just happens to have a press corps camped out watching. Either way, if you want a soap opera with actual policy implications, this is your town.

What You'll Find on This Site

If any of that got you curious, good — that's the point of this site. So having successfully manipulated your emotions in to morbid curiosity consider this your invitation to explore.

You can check out our History — the deep dive into how a swampy midpoint between Pensacola and St. Augustine turned into a state capital, from land grants and railroads to banks that somehow also used to be feed stores and shoe factories. It's got Napoleon's nephew, a failed silk colony, and a mayor who called the local racetrack "a hotbed of vice." It's a lot.

Or there's Politics — a closer look at how this small city ended up running the whole state, why Leon County votes differently than its neighbors, and the cast of characters (Askew, DeSantis, Gillum, and more) who've called the Capitol their office. Consider it the extended, less-humorous cousin of the section you just read.

And finally, there's Zombieland. Our most beloved resident, favorite son, and honestly the only Tallahasseean who's ever properly killed a zombie with a banjo (look it up). Yep THE Tallahassee gets his own page, because when your city and a badass Woody Harrelson character share a name, you don't waste that. You lean all the way in.
